Donor Prospecting with Salesforce: A Data-Driven Approach

Food-N-Force Team November 2025 4 min read

The difference between a food bank that raises $800,000 per year and one that raises $2 million is rarely the size of their donor base. More often it's the depth of their prospecting practice — their ability to systematically identify who among their extended network of supporters, volunteers, and community contacts has both the capacity and the inclination to give significantly. Salesforce, configured thoughtfully, is one of the most powerful tools available for building that practice.

Donor prospecting doesn't mean cold outreach. It means using the data your organization has already accumulated — giving history, event attendance, volunteer engagement, email response rates — to identify who deserves proactive, personal cultivation. Done well, it feels less like fundraising and more like relationship building at scale. Salesforce provides the infrastructure; your development team provides the judgment and the human connection.

Defining Your Ideal Donor Profile

Effective prospecting begins with a clear picture of who your most committed donors actually are. Before you can identify future major donors, you need to understand the characteristics that distinguish your current ones. Pull a report of your top donors over the past three years: when did they first give? What was their pathway to a major gift — were they longtime small donors, event attendees, volunteers, or people who came in cold? What programs or impact areas seem to resonate most with them?

This analysis, even if informal, produces a rough ideal donor profile: the combination of engagement history, interest areas, and relationship characteristics that correlates with deep commitment to your mission. In Salesforce, you can begin tagging contacts who match this profile — volunteers with multiple years of service who haven't yet been asked to give, regular event attendees who opened every email campaign, board members' personal contacts who've made small gifts and never been followed up with. This becomes your primary prospecting universe.

Mining Your Existing Data

Your Salesforce database almost certainly contains more prospecting intelligence than you're currently using. Campaign member records show who opened emails and who clicked links — a reliable proxy for mission engagement. Event registration history shows who shows up and how often. Volunteer logs show sustained investment of time, which often precedes investment of money. Household records with multiple giving contacts may indicate family foundations or estate planning opportunities.

Build a Salesforce report that surfaces contacts meeting multiple engagement criteria simultaneously: gave at least once in the past 24 months AND attended an event in the past 12 months AND opened at least 3 email campaigns. Filter out anyone already assigned to a major gifts portfolio. The result is a shortlist of warm prospects who have demonstrated genuine interest but haven't yet been meaningfully engaged at a major donor level. This is where your prospecting time is best spent.

Donor prospecting done well feels less like fundraising and more like relationship building at scale — Salesforce provides the infrastructure, your team provides the human connection.

List Segmentation and Lead Scoring

Not all prospects deserve the same level of personal attention. Segmentation allows you to prioritize your outreach based on the combination of capacity indicators and engagement signals you've identified. In Salesforce, this means using a combination of native fields, custom fields, and campaign assignments to segment your prospect list into tiers: prospects ready for immediate personal outreach, prospects who need more cultivation before a conversation, and prospects who are better served by continued mass communications until they demonstrate stronger signals.

Lead scoring — a concept borrowed from sales CRM practice — can be adapted for nonprofit prospecting. Assign point values to engagement behaviors: a donation (even small) earns points, event attendance earns points, volunteer hours earn points, email clicks earn points. Set thresholds for when a contact should move between segments. This can be maintained manually for smaller organizations or automated via Flow Builder for those with larger prospect pools. The goal is not to reduce donors to numbers but to ensure your team's personal attention goes to the people most likely to respond to it.

Wealth Screening Integration

Salesforce integrates with several wealth screening and donor research platforms that append publicly available capacity indicators — real estate holdings, business affiliations, philanthropy history — to your contact records. This data helps distinguish between engaged contacts with limited giving capacity and those who could be major donors if properly cultivated.

Wealth screening is most useful when used as a triage tool rather than a targeting tool. It helps you prioritize which warm prospects deserve the investment of a personal ask conversation — not which people are "worth" cultivating. Many of your most committed future donors will not show up in wealth screening tools because their capacity comes through business equity, inheritance, or estate assets that aren't publicly tracked. Use the data to inform your priorities; don't let it narrow your vision of who your mission can reach.

Building and Managing Your Cultivation Pipeline

Once you have a segmented prospect list, you need a system for managing individual cultivation tracks. In Salesforce, this typically means using Opportunity records to represent major gift prospects — even before a specific ask amount or timeline is determined. Opportunities allow you to track prospect stage (Identified, Qualified, Cultivating, Ready to Solicit, Solicited, Closed), document touchpoints via Activity records, and run pipeline reports that give leadership visibility into where development effort is focused.

The most important discipline in pipeline management is consistent activity logging. A cultivation pipeline with no activity records is a list of names, not a managed program. Every meaningful interaction — a personal email, a phone call, a coffee meeting, a tour of the facility — should be logged as a Task or Event on the prospect's record. Over time, this activity history becomes invaluable: when a prospect eventually makes a major gift, the record tells the full story of the relationship. When a prospect goes cold, the activity log reveals why and what re-engagement might look like.

Tracking Multi-Touch Engagement

Modern donor cultivation happens across many touchpoints: email, social media, events, personal outreach, programmatic experiences like volunteer shifts or facility tours. Salesforce's Campaign and Campaign Member functionality allows you to track donor engagement across all of these — building a comprehensive picture of how each prospect is interacting with your organization over time. Use Campaign Influence features in Nonprofit Cloud to understand which touchpoints are most correlated with gift decisions. This data informs future cultivation strategy and helps justify investment in engagement programs that might otherwise be hard to measure. Over time, a well-maintained Salesforce org doesn't just support your current prospecting program — it compounds in value as each year's engagement data adds to the longitudinal picture of who your donors are and what they respond to.
