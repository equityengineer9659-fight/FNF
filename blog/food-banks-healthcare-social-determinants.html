Food Banks and Healthcare: Addressing Social Determinants of Health

Food-N-Force Team September 2025 5 min read

Food insecurity is no longer just a hunger issue — it is a clinical one. Health systems across the country are now screening patients for food insecurity as a standard of care, and the referrals they generate are landing at food bank doors. For food organizations prepared to receive them, this convergence opens a new era of funding, data, and community health impact. For those that are not, it creates pressure they cannot manage.

The framework driving this shift is social determinants of health (SDOH) — the non-medical factors that influence health outcomes. Housing, transportation, income, and food access are the most powerful predictors of chronic disease risk, hospital readmission rates, and overall health system costs. Food insecurity consistently ranks among the top tier of these drivers, with strong associations to diabetes progression, cardiovascular disease, and maternal health outcomes.

What SDOH Means for Food Banks

When health systems integrate SDOH screening into clinical workflows, they use standardized tools — the most widely adopted being the Hunger Vital Sign, a two-question screener validated for identifying household food insecurity. When a patient screens positive, the clinical team generates a referral. In communities with strong food bank partnerships, that referral is warm: a direct connection to a specific pantry or food program with an appointment, not just a list of phone numbers.

This is a significant operational change for food banks. Clinic-referred clients arrive differently than walk-ins. They may have specific dietary restrictions tied to a diagnosis. They expect a private, dignified experience. They may be connected through an electronic health record system that shares limited data with your intake process. Being ready to serve this population well requires advance preparation — not just additional shelf capacity.

What Food Banks Gain from Healthcare Partnerships

The benefits of healthcare partnership are substantial. Health systems bring institutional funding sources that food banks rarely access directly — Medicaid managed care contracts, community benefit dollars from hospital systems required to demonstrate community health investment, and grant programs tied to population health management. A formal partnership with a health system changes the funding conversations available to your organization.

Referral volume is another concrete gain. Health system partnerships create a reliable, consistent stream of new clients who are already identified as food insecure — removing the outreach burden of finding people who need services. And when your programs are connected to clinical care, your outcomes data becomes relevant to health system quality metrics. That relevance is leverage in future funding negotiations.

Health systems pay close attention to programs that reduce unnecessary emergency department visits. A food bank that can show its services contributed to that outcome has a partner, not just a referral source.

Navigating HIPAA and Data Sharing

Healthcare partnerships introduce regulatory complexity that food banks have not historically had to manage. HIPAA governs how health information can be shared, and its implications for food organizations are nuanced. The referral itself — the fact that a patient was screened positive and referred — does not automatically carry protected health information. But when health systems want to close the loop by receiving service confirmation back from the food bank, or when clinical diagnoses are shared to enable medically tailored food programming, a formal data sharing agreement becomes necessary.

Most food banks are not covered entities under HIPAA, which means they are not bound by HIPAA directly. But the health systems they partner with are, and those systems must have Business Associate Agreements or other authorized mechanisms before sharing patient data externally. Working through these agreements takes time and legal review. Build that lead time into your partnership planning.

Separately, food banks should review their own data privacy practices with client data before entering healthcare partnerships. Clients referred by health systems have elevated expectations of confidentiality — and the trust implications of any breach extend to both organizations.

Technology That Supports Bidirectional Referrals

The operational heart of a healthcare partnership is a closed-loop referral process. The clinic screens and refers. The food bank receives, serves, and confirms. The health system sees the outcome. Without technology, this loop requires phone calls, faxes, and staff coordination that does not scale. With the right systems, it becomes automated.

Platforms like Unite Us, Aunt Bertha (now findhelp), and 211 systems provide community resource referral infrastructure that many health systems already use. Connecting your Salesforce-based intake records to these platforms allows inbound referrals to populate client records automatically and outbound service confirmations to close the loop without manual entry. If your health system partner uses Epic or Cerner, direct integration via FHIR APIs is increasingly feasible for larger organizations.

For food banks earlier in their technology journey, even a structured intake form with fields for referral source and health conditions — combined with a weekly report back to the health system partner — is a meaningful start. The goal is demonstrating that your organization can reliably close the loop. That demonstration is what earns expanded partnership and funding.

Measuring Outcomes for Healthcare Funders

Healthcare funders measure impact differently than traditional food banking funders. They are less interested in pounds distributed and more interested in clinical outcomes: HbA1c reductions among diabetic clients, emergency department utilization changes, or self-reported food security scores measured before and after program enrollment. They want to see a theory of change that connects your food programming to the health metric they care about.

This does not mean food banks need to become clinical research organizations. It means capturing structured data that can be analyzed for health-relevant patterns: client health conditions at intake, changes in food security score over time, and participation rates in specific nutrition programs. With even basic pre/post measurement, you can build a credible outcomes case that speaks to healthcare funders in their own terms.

The convergence of food banking and healthcare is not a temporary trend — it reflects a durable shift in how the health system understands the relationship between food and health. Food banks that build the operational readiness, data infrastructure, and partnership relationships to participate in this convergence will find themselves at the center of a growing funding stream and a much broader definition of what hunger relief organizations can accomplish.
