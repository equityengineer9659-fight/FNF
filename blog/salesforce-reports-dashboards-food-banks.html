Salesforce Reports and Dashboards Every Food Bank Leader Needs

Food-N-Force Team July 2025 5 min read

Data does not drive decisions. Accessible, well-designed data drives decisions. Most food banks collect more information than they can use — but leaders still find themselves without the numbers they need when board meetings arrive or grant deadlines loom. Salesforce reports and dashboards solve this problem when they are built deliberately around the questions leaders actually ask, not around the data that happens to be easy to display.

Reports vs. Dashboards: A Useful Distinction

In Salesforce, reports are structured queries — they answer specific questions about your data. A report can tell you exactly how many clients visited each distribution site last month, broken down by zip code. Dashboards are visual summaries that aggregate multiple reports into a single view. Think of reports as the engine and dashboards as the instrument panel. Leaders typically work from dashboards for day-to-day visibility; they drill into reports when they need to investigate a trend or pull data for a specific purpose.

Salesforce Nonprofit Success Pack and Nonprofit Cloud both include built-in report types for Contacts, Opportunities (donations), Campaigns, and Cases. Custom report types can be added to surface program-specific data. Before building anything, audit what report types are already available in your org — starting from existing infrastructure is faster than building from scratch.

Six Essential Reports for Food Banks

The first report every food bank needs is client visits by week and month. Built on a custom object or Cases, filtered by service type and grouped by date, this report shows volume trends at a glance. Add a comparison to the prior period — same month last year — and you have context for interpreting whether changes reflect seasonal patterns or something requiring action.

Inventory turnover reporting tracks how quickly food categories move through your warehouse. A high-level version groups inventory movements by product category and calculates average days-on-hand. This report is most valuable for identifying categories with chronic over-supply (reducing waste) and under-supply (informing procurement).

Volunteer hours by program, built on Salesforce's Volunteers for Salesforce package or a custom volunteer object, shows where volunteer capacity is concentrated and where gaps exist. Grouping by program area and comparing to volunteer hour targets is essential for grant reporting and for making the case to volunteer coordinators about where recruitment should focus.

Donation revenue by source — an Opportunity report grouped by campaign or record type — is foundational for fundraising teams. Filtering by Close Date gives you period-specific revenue figures. Adding a Probability field filter to exclude closed-lost records keeps your pipeline view clean. This report feeds grant reporting, board finance updates, and cash flow projections.

Grant reporting summary pulls all Opportunities of record type "Grant" with relevant fields: funder name, amount awarded, reporting deadline, and associated campaign. Sorted by reporting deadline, this report functions as a grants calendar, making it easy to see which reports are due in the next 30, 60, or 90 days.

Distribution by site, built as a matrix report with sites as rows and time periods as columns, shows which locations are serving the most clients and where growth is occurring. This report is particularly useful for multi-site operations making decisions about resource allocation, hours, and expansion.

Building the Executive Dashboard

An executive dashboard should answer the question any leader has when they open their laptop on a Monday morning: how are we doing? Design it to fit on a single screen without scrolling. Aim for six to ten components maximum. More than that and the dashboard stops being a leadership tool and becomes a data exploration interface — useful, but not the same thing.

Reports are the engine; dashboards are the instrument panel. Leaders typically work from dashboards for day-to-day visibility and drill into reports when they need to investigate a trend.

Standard components for a food bank executive dashboard: a gauge or metric tile showing clients served this month versus goal, a bar chart showing distribution volume by week for the rolling 12 weeks, a donut chart showing donation revenue by source year-to-date, a table showing open grants with nearest reporting deadlines, a metric tile for active volunteers this month, and a table showing distribution events scheduled in the next two weeks. Each component links to the underlying report so leaders can drill into detail without leaving the dashboard.

Use dashboard filters to allow leaders to slice the same dashboard by fiscal year, program area, or geographic region. Building one flexible dashboard is more maintainable than building six specialized ones.

Scheduling Reports and Dashboard Subscriptions

Salesforce allows reports to be scheduled for automatic email delivery — daily, weekly, or monthly. For operational leaders who need weekly visit counts or monthly revenue totals, scheduled reports eliminate the need to log in and run the report manually. Set up scheduled delivery for your highest-frequency reports first.

Dashboard subscriptions send a snapshot of a dashboard to designated recipients on a schedule. This feature is ideal for board members who need regular visibility but do not need a full Salesforce license. A weekly dashboard subscription delivered to the executive director and board chair keeps leadership informed without requiring them to navigate the system. Combine dashboard subscriptions with Chatter posts that contextualize notable data points and you have a simple but effective leadership communication cadence.

Sharing Dashboards with Board Members

Board members require visibility into organizational performance, but most do not need — and should not have — full Salesforce access. Salesforce's read-only license type provides dashboard and report viewing at a fraction of the cost of full licenses. Alternatively, Salesforce Experience Cloud can expose specific dashboards to external users through a branded portal.

Before sharing dashboards externally, review what data is visible. Client-identifiable information should never appear in board-facing dashboards — aggregate metrics only. Apply field-level security and sharing rules to ensure that external viewers see only the aggregated program and financial data appropriate to their role.

Keeping Dashboards Actionable

The most common dashboard failure is information overload. Organizations build comprehensive dashboards that display everything they can measure, and leaders stop looking at them because they require too much interpretation. Every component on a dashboard should correspond to a decision or action. If a metric triggers no possible response from the person viewing it, it does not belong on the dashboard.

Review your dashboards quarterly. Remove components that leaders consistently ignore. Update targets when goals change. Archive dashboards from completed programs. A well-maintained, focused dashboard used by every leader beats a comprehensive data environment that no one opens. Start small, build confidence, and expand based on the questions leadership actually asks.
