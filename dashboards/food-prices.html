Food Prices by Category

Not All Prices Rise Equally

Since 2020, food prices have surged unevenly across categories. Cereals and bakery products led the spike, rising over 24% in just two years. Dairy prices were the most volatile, swinging wildly before stabilizing at elevated levels.

Meanwhile, fruits and vegetables — the foods nutritionists recommend most — have consistently been the most expensive category, putting healthy diets further out of reach for low-income families.

Source Bureau of Labor Statistics, Consumer Price Index Updated Monthly (live data via BLS API) Baseline CPI Index (1982-84 = 100)
Cereals & bakery products saw the steepest single-year increase: 12% from Jan 2022 to Jan 2023.

Regional Price Comparison

Where You Live Determines What You Pay

The West and Northeast consistently have the highest food-at-home prices, driven by higher housing costs, transportation expenses, and labor markets. The South has the lowest prices — but also the lowest median incomes, making food no more affordable.

Regional CPI differences have widened since 2020, with the West pulling further ahead.

Source BLS CPI, Regional Food at Home indexes Data Range 2020-2025 (semi-annual) Regions Northeast, Midwest, South, West
The West pays 8.6% more for food at home than the South — a gap that has grown by 2 percentage points since 2020.

Price vs. What You Earn

Affordability is not just about price — it is about price relative to income. This map shows a food affordability index: higher values mean food costs consume a larger share of the state's median income.

Southern states with low food prices still rank poorly because of low median incomes. Meanwhile, states like Maryland and New Hampshire combine moderate prices with high incomes for the best affordability.

Source Feeding America meal costs + Census ACS median household income Data Year 2022 Index (Meal cost / Median income) x 1,000,000 — higher = less affordable
Louisiana, West Virginia, and Mississippi are the least affordable despite having the lowest raw food prices in the country.

Cost Burden by Income

The Poorest Pay the Most

The lowest-income 20% of American households spend 31.2% of their income on food — more than four times the rate of the wealthiest quintile. When food prices rise, these families have nowhere to cut. They are already spending the minimum needed to eat.

A 10% increase in food prices costs a low-income family an extra $40/month — the equivalent of 10 missed meals at the average meal cost of $3.99.

Source USDA ERS, Food Expenditure Series Data Year 2022 Metric % of pre-tax income spent on food at home + away
The bottom quintile spends $407/month on food — compared to $1,028 for the top quintile. Less money, worse nutrition.

Food at Home vs. Away Trends

Cooking at Home: Still the Budget Option?

For decades, cooking at home was the clear budget choice. The 2022-2023 inflation surge changed that — food-at-home prices spiked faster than restaurant prices for the first time in modern history. The gap has since narrowed, but grocery bills remain elevated.

Use the slider below the chart to zoom into specific time periods. The "All Items" dashed line shows general inflation for comparison.

Source Bureau of Labor Statistics, CPI Updated Monthly (live data via BLS API) Baseline CPI Index (1982-84 = 100)
Food-at-home prices rose 25% from 2020-2024 — outpacing general inflation by 5 percentage points.
