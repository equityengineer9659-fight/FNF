Food Prices by Category

Not All Prices Rise Equally

Since 2020, food prices have surged unevenly across categories. Cereals and bakery products led the spike, rising over 24% in just two years. Dairy prices were the most volatile, swinging wildly before stabilizing at elevated levels.

Meanwhile, fruits and vegetables — the foods nutritionists recommend most — have consistently been the most expensive category. Nonalcoholic beverages and other food at home (fats, oils, sugars) round out the full picture of grocery inflation, putting healthy diets further out of reach for low-income families.

Source Bureau of Labor Statistics, Consumer Price Index Updated Monthly (live data via BLS API) Baseline CPI Index (1982-84 = 100)
Cereals & bakery products saw the steepest single-year increase: ~10% from Jan 2022 to Jan 2023.

Regional Price Comparison

Where You Live Determines What You Pay

The West and Northeast consistently have the highest food-at-home prices, associated with higher housing costs, transportation expenses, and labor markets. The South has the lowest prices — but also the lowest median incomes, making food no more affordable.

Regional CPI differences have remained relatively stable since 2020, with the West consistently paying the most.

Source BLS CPI, Regional Food at Home indexes Data Range 2018-present (monthly via BLS API) Regions Northeast, Midwest, South, West
The West pays approximately 7% more for food at home than the South — a persistent gap associated with higher costs of living.

Price vs. What You Earn

Affordability is not just about price — it is about price relative to income. This map shows a food affordability index: higher values mean food costs consume a larger share of the state's median income.

Southern states with low food prices still rank poorly because of low median incomes. Meanwhile, states like Maryland and New Hampshire combine moderate prices with high incomes for the best affordability.

Source Feeding America meal costs + Census ACS median household income Data Year 2024 Index Annual 3-meal food cost per $1,000 of median income — higher = less affordable
Louisiana, West Virginia, and Mississippi are the least affordable despite having the lowest raw food prices in the country.

Cost Burden by Income

The Poorest Pay the Most

The lowest-income 20% of American households spend 32.6% of their income on food — more than four times the rate of the wealthiest quintile. When food prices rise, these families have nowhere to cut. They are already spending the minimum needed to eat.

A 10% increase in food prices costs a low-income family an extra $44/month — the equivalent of 12 missed meals at the average meal cost of $3.58.

Source USDA ERS, Food Expenditure Series Data Year 2022 (latest available; USDA ERS typically releases on a 2-year lag) Metric % of pre-tax income spent on food at home + away
The bottom quintile spends $440/month on food — compared to $1,416 for the top quintile. Less money, worse nutrition.

Food at Home vs. Away Trends

Cooking at Home: Still the Budget Option?

For decades, cooking at home was the clear budget choice. The 2022-2023 inflation surge changed that — food-at-home prices spiked faster than restaurant prices for the first time in modern history. The gap has since narrowed, but grocery bills remain elevated.

Use the slider below the chart to zoom into specific time periods. The "All Items" dashed line shows general inflation for comparison.

Source Bureau of Labor Statistics, CPI Updated Monthly (live data via BLS API) Baseline CPI Index (1982-84 = 100)
Food-at-home prices rose ~28% from 2020 to 2024 — outpacing general inflation by 5 percentage points.

Food Inflation Rate (Year-over-Year)

The Number That Makes Headlines

"Food prices rose 11.4% in 2022" is instantly understandable. Raw CPI index values are not. This chart shows the year-over-year percentage change — the number policymakers, journalists, and grant writers actually cite.

The spike above 10% in mid-2022 was the highest food inflation since the early 1980s. By 2024, the rate fell sharply from the 2022 peak — but prices remain at the elevated plateau, with the rate at 3.1% as of early 2026.

Source Bureau of Labor Statistics, CPI (computed from monthly index)
Updated Monthly via BLS API
Calculation (Current month CPI − same month prior year) ÷ prior year × 100

Are SNAP Benefits Keeping Up?

SNAP vs. Food Prices: Purchasing Power Index

This chart indexes both food-at-home prices and average SNAP monthly benefits to a common 2018 baseline (= 100). When the food price line rises faster than the SNAP benefit line, families on SNAP are losing purchasing power.

The Thrifty Food Plan reevaluation in October 2021 boosted SNAP benefits significantly. The 2022 inflation surge and end of emergency allotments in March 2023 created a temporary purchasing power gap — but current benefit levels have recovered and now outpace food price growth on the 2018 index: SNAP +55% vs. food +37%.

Use the item toggles below the chart to overlay individual food prices (eggs, bread, ground beef, dairy) sourced from the FRED API — see which staples have outpaced benefits the most.

Source BLS CPI (food prices) + USDA FNS (SNAP benefits) + FRED (item CPI)
Baseline January 2018 = 100 for both series

Prices vs. Food Insecurity

When Prices Rise, Hunger Follows

This chart overlays monthly food prices (CPI) with annual food insecurity rates to reveal the strong temporal relationship. The 2022 food price spike directly preceded the sharpest increase in food insecurity since 2008.

While prices have decelerated since 2023, they remain permanently elevated — and food insecurity has not retreated.

Sources BLS Consumer Price Index + USDA ERS Food Insecurity Report Note Insecurity data is annual (plotted at January); CPI is monthly
