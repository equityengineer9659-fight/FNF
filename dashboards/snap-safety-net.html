SNAP Participation Trend

A Decade of SNAP

SNAP enrollment was declining steadily from 2015 to early 2020 as the economy improved. Then COVID-19 changed everything — enrollment surged from 37 million to over 43 million in just months. Emergency allotments boosted benefits through 2023.

When emergency benefits ended in March 2023, millions of families saw their monthly benefits cut by $90-$250. Enrollment stabilized near 42 million through mid-2025, but has since declined sharply — falling from 42.8M in January 2025 to 40.4M by November 2025, a 5.6% drop coinciding with USDA policy changes and a government shutdown.

Source USDA FNS, SNAP Data Tables (FY2025 data as of March 2026) Updated Monthly from USDA FNS (through Nov 2025) Unit Millions of participants
Policy markers show key inflection points: COVID emergency allotments, the Thrifty Food Plan update, the end of emergency benefits, and — new in late 2025 — the USDA terminating its food security report and removing the SNAP contingency plan, coinciding with a government shutdown. The dashed Food CPI overlay (when available) shows economic pressure driving enrollment.

Where Is the Safety Net Strongest?

This map shows the SNAP-to-need ratio — the percentage of food-insecure people who are receiving SNAP benefits. A ratio above 100% means SNAP is reaching beyond the food-insecure population (covering people at risk).

States with strong outreach programs like Illinois, New York, and Oregon exceed 100% coverage. States like Wyoming, Utah, and Kansas have significant gaps.

Toggle to "Self-Reported (CDC)" to compare administrative SNAP data with the CDC PLACES BRFSS survey, where adults self-report receiving food stamps. The gap between administrative and self-reported rates can reveal stigma effects — states where self-reported rates fall below administrative rates may have populations reluctant to disclose SNAP receipt.

Source USDA FNS SNAP Data + Feeding America | CDC PLACES BRFSS (toggle) Data Year 2022 (FNS) | 2023 (CDC PLACES) Metric Administrative: SNAP participants / food-insecure pop | CDC: % adults self-reporting food stamp receipt
Wyoming has the lowest administrative coverage ratio at 40.6%. When CDC self-reported data loads, compare the two views — states where self-reported is notably lower than administrative suggest stigma or under-reporting of benefits.

Safety Net Coverage Flow

Who Falls Through the Cracks?

This flow diagram traces 44.2 million food-insecure Americans from who they are (children, seniors, working adults), through which safety net program catches them, to whether their food needs are actually met.

10.5 million people receive no assistance at all — and nearly all of them remain in crisis. Even among those enrolled in SNAP, only 60% have their food needs fully met. Working-age adults are the most underserved: 8 million have zero safety net coverage.

Source USDA FNS, Feeding America, Census ACS (modeled estimates) Data Year 2022 Read as Left → Middle → Right: who they are → what helps them → whether it's enough
12.7 million Americans remain in food crisis — and 83% of them either have no program or rely solely on food banks.

School Lunch Reach

School Lunch: The Most Reliable Meal

For 29.6 million children, the school lunch program may be the most reliable meal of the day. In Mississippi, over 75% of students qualify for free or reduced-price lunch. Nationally, more than half of all students depend on this program.

Hover over any state to see the free vs. reduced-price breakdown. Free lunch goes to families below 130% of the Federal Poverty Level; reduced-price covers 130-185% FPL. Nationally, 85% of participants receive free meals and 15% pay the reduced price — meaning the vast majority of children in this program live in deep poverty.

Source USDA FNS Child Nutrition Tables + KIDS COUNT Data Year FY2023 school year Metric % students receiving free or reduced-price lunch (with breakdown)
12 states have over 60% of students on free/reduced lunch — concentrated in the South and Appalachia. In the highest-need states like Mississippi and Louisiana, nearly 90% of qualifying students receive free (not reduced-price) meals, reflecting deeper poverty concentration.

SNAP Benefits Per Person

Are Benefits Enough?

The average SNAP benefit is $188 per person per month — about $6.27 per day, or $2.09 per meal. Benefits vary by state, household size, and income, but the central question remains: is this enough to eat?

The food insecurity rate line overlay shows whether higher benefits correlate with lower insecurity. The relationship is complex — states with high benefits often have high costs of living too.

Source USDA FNS SNAP State Activity Reports Data Year FY2025 Metric Average monthly benefit per person ($)
Hawaii's $312/month benefit is the highest — but its $5.12 average meal cost makes it less adequate than it appears.

Safety Net at a Glance

The Executive Summary

SNAP Coverage — 83.7% of food-insecure Americans receive SNAP benefits. The remaining 8.2 million fall through the gap, often linked to eligibility rules, stigma, or lack of outreach.

School Lunch — 52.1% of students nationally qualify for free or reduced-price meals. For many children, this is the most reliable meal of the day.

Avg Benefit — $188/month per person, or about $2.09 per meal. Benefits vary by state, household size, and income.

Monthly Shortfall — The gap between SNAP benefits ($188) and the cost of 3 meals/day at the national average meal cost ($3.58). Families must cover this difference from other income.

Source USDA FNS SNAP Data Tables & Feeding America
Data Year FY2025 (national estimates, as of March 2026)
Data Static (from snap-participation.json)
These four numbers are the ones grant writers cite most. Copy them directly into proposals — they come from authoritative federal sources.

Who Falls Through the Safety Net?

Equity in the Safety Net

Food insecurity does not affect all communities equally. Black and Hispanic Americans face food insecurity at 2-3x the rate of White Americans — linked to systemic disparities in income, employment, and wealth.

This flow diagram traces how each demographic group moves through the safety net: from population share to food insecurity to coverage or gaps. For federal grants, equity analysis is not optional — it is required.
