SNAP Participation Trend

A Decade of SNAP

SNAP enrollment was declining steadily from 2015 to early 2020 as the economy improved. Then COVID-19 changed everything — enrollment surged from 37 million to over 43 million in just months. Emergency allotments boosted benefits through 2023.

When emergency benefits ended in March 2023, millions of families saw their monthly benefits cut by $90-$250. Enrollment has stabilized around 42 million — still well above pre-pandemic levels.

Source USDA Food and Nutrition Service, SNAP Data Tables Updated Monthly from USDA FNS Unit Millions of participants
Policy markers show key inflection points: COVID emergency allotments, the Thrifty Food Plan update, and the end of emergency benefits.

SNAP Coverage by State

Where Is the Safety Net Strongest?

This map shows the SNAP-to-need ratio — the percentage of food-insecure people who are receiving SNAP benefits. A ratio above 100% means SNAP is reaching beyond the food-insecure population (covering people at risk).

States with strong outreach programs like Illinois, New York, and Oregon exceed 100% coverage. States like Wyoming, Utah, and Kansas have significant gaps.

Source USDA FNS SNAP Data + Feeding America Data Year 2022 Metric SNAP participants / food-insecure population x 100
Wyoming has the lowest coverage ratio at 40.6% — nearly 60% of food-insecure residents are not receiving SNAP.

Coverage Gap Analysis

Millions Falling Through the Cracks

Across the top 15 states by food insecurity, millions of people are food insecure but not receiving SNAP. The gap is driven by eligibility thresholds, application barriers, stigma, and lack of awareness.

Texas has the largest absolute coverage gap: 4.5 million food-insecure residents but only 3.5 million SNAP participants. That is over 1 million people without safety net coverage.

Source USDA FNS + Feeding America Map the Meal Gap Data Year 2022
Texas, Indiana, and Virginia have the largest gaps between food-insecure populations and SNAP enrollment.

School Lunch Reach

School Lunch: The Most Reliable Meal

For 30 million children, the school lunch program may be the most reliable meal of the day. In Mississippi, over 75% of students qualify for free or reduced-price lunch. Nationally, more than half of all students depend on this program.

The national average line shows the dividing point: states above it have higher child poverty and food insecurity. Summer months, when school is out, create a dangerous gap.

Source USDA FNS, National School Lunch Program + KIDS COUNT Data Year 2023 school year Metric % students receiving free or reduced-price lunch
8 states have over 60% of students on free/reduced lunch — all in the Deep South or Southwest.

SNAP Benefits Per Person

Are Benefits Enough?

The average SNAP benefit is $194 per person per month — about $6.47 per day, or $2.16 per meal. Benefits vary by state, household size, and income, but the central question remains: is this enough to eat?

The food insecurity rate line overlay shows whether higher benefits correlate with lower insecurity. The relationship is complex — states with high benefits often have high costs of living too.

Source USDA FNS SNAP State Activity Reports Data Year 2024 Metric Average monthly benefit per person ($)
Hawaii's $312/month benefit is the highest — but its $5.12 average meal cost makes it less adequate than it appears.

Strengthening the Safety Net

Technology plays a critical role in closing coverage gaps. Food banks that connect their operations with SNAP outreach, school lunch coordination, and community referrals can help more families access the benefits they qualify for. Food-N-Force builds these integrations on Salesforce — helping organizations bridge the gap between need and access.
