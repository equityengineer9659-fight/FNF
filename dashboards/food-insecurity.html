    The Scale of the Problem

    In 2022, food insecurity surged to its highest level in nearly a decade. More than 44 million Americans — including 13 million children — didn't know where their next meal would come from. That's roughly 1 in 8 people across the country.

    Where Hunger Hits Hardest

    Food insecurity isn't evenly distributed. The Deep South — Mississippi, Louisiana, Arkansas, and New Mexico — faces rates nearly double the national average. But even wealthy states have pockets of severe need. Click any state above to see the county-level picture.

    A Decade of Progress — Then Reversal

    From 2013 to 2021, the nation made steady progress — food insecurity dropped from 14.3% to 10.2%. Then in 2022, rising food prices, expiring pandemic-era benefits, and inflation pushed millions back into hunger. The child food insecurity rate jumped from 12.5% to 17.9% in a single year.

    Poverty Drives Hunger

    The scatter plot above tells a clear story: where poverty is high, food insecurity follows. But the relationship isn't one-to-one — states with higher meal costs can have food insecurity rates that exceed what poverty alone would predict. The cost of eating matters as much as income.
