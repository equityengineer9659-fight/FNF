    The Scale of the Problem

    In 2022, food insecurity surged to its highest level in nearly a decade. More than 44 million Americans — including 13 million children — didn't know where their next meal would come from.

    The Deep South faces rates nearly double the national average. Click any state to see county-level data.

    Source USDA ERS & Feeding America Map the Meal Gap 2024
    Data Year 2022
    Updated USDA report discontinued after 2024 edition. Feeding America continues annually (next: Spring 2026)
    Granularity State & county level
    Mississippi leads the nation at 18.7% — nearly 1 in 5 residents.

    National Trend (2013-2022)

    A Decade of Progress — Then Reversal

    From 2013 to 2021, the nation made steady progress — food insecurity dropped from 14.3% to 10.2%. Then in 2022, rising food prices, expiring pandemic-era benefits, and inflation pushed millions back into hunger.

    The child food insecurity rate jumped from 12.5% to 17.9% in a single year.

    Source USDA ERS, Current Population Survey Food Security Supplement
    Updated USDA report was annual (September). Discontinued after Dec 2025 release. Last available: 2024 data
    2022 reversed nearly a decade of progress in a single year.

    Poverty vs. Food Insecurity

    Poverty Drives Hunger

    Where poverty is high, food insecurity follows — but the relationship isn't one-to-one. States with higher meal costs can have food insecurity rates that exceed what poverty alone would predict. The cost of eating matters as much as income.

    Each dot represents one state. Dot size reflects the number of food-insecure persons.

    Source U.S. Census Bureau ACS (poverty) & USDA ERS (food insecurity)
    Data Year 2022 (5-year ACS estimates)
    Updated ACS: annually each December (next: Dec 2026). USDA ERS: discontinued after 2024 data
    Strong positive correlation (r ≈ 0.85) — poverty is the #1 predictor of food insecurity.

    Social Determinants of Food Insecurity

    Beyond Poverty: What Drives Hunger?

    Poverty is the strongest predictor, but other social determinants compound the risk. States with high unemployment, low education, high uninsured rates, limited vehicle access, and severe housing cost burden tend to have higher food insecurity — even after accounting for income.

    Use the buttons to explore how each factor correlates with food insecurity.

    Children Bear the Heaviest Burden

    The Child Vulnerability Gap

    In every state, children face higher food insecurity rates than the general population. The gap ranges from 4 to 8 percentage points — meaning children are 30-50% more likely to be food insecure than adults in the same state.

    For grant writers: child food insecurity is the single most funded dimension of hunger research. These rates come directly from USDA and Feeding America surveys.

    Source USDA ERS & Feeding America, Map the Meal Gap 2024
    Data Year 2022

    Income & Hunger: The River of Inequality

    Follow the Money

    States are sorted left-to-right by food insecurity rate. As insecurity rises, the low-income bands widen and high-income bands narrow — a visual proof that income distribution is the structural driver of hunger.

    The river makes the policy case: states with more households under $25K consistently have higher food insecurity. Targeted income support programs have the highest ROI for hunger reduction.

    Average Meal Cost by State

    The Cost of a Meal

    The average cost of a meal varies by nearly 60% across states — from $3.18 in Mississippi to $5.12 in Hawaii. Higher meal costs mean food bank dollars stretch less, and families need more income to avoid food insecurity.

    Source Feeding America, Map the Meal Gap 2024
    Methodology Weighted average of USDA Thrifty Food Plan costs
    Updated Annually with Map the Meal Gap (next: Spring 2026 with 2024 data)
    Hawaii's $5.12/meal is 28% above the national average — and 61% above Mississippi's $3.18, the lowest in the nation.

    How We Define Food Insecurity

    A household is food insecure when it lacks consistent access to enough food for every person to live an active, healthy life. The USDA measures this through an 18-question survey module administered annually. "Low food security" and "very low food security" are both counted.

    County-Level Modeling

    County food insecurity rates are modeled from Census Bureau poverty data using a regression model (food insecurity ≈ 0.75 × poverty rate + 2.5), validated against Feeding America's published county estimates. Child rates use a 1.3-1.6x multiplier based on state-level child-to-overall ratios.

    Data Currency

    State-level food insecurity data reflects 2022. The USDA discontinued its annual food security report after the 2024 edition (released Dec 2025). Feeding America's Map the Meal Gap continues annually. BLS food prices update monthly via live API. Census ACS 5-year estimates release each December.
