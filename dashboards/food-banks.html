Where Are the Food Banks?

This map shows food assistance organizations per 100,000 residents — a measure of infrastructure density. States with higher density have more organizations available to serve their population.

Vermont and Maine lead with over 20 organizations per 100K, while Nevada and Utah have among the fewest. High density does not always mean adequate capacity — small pantries count the same as large regional food banks.

Source ProPublica Nonprofit Explorer (IRS 990 filings, NTEE K31) Data Year 2023 filing year Metric Organizations per 100,000 residents
DC has the highest density (35.9 per 100K) but is an outlier due to its small population and high number of national headquarters.

Resources vs. Need

Do Resources Match Need?

In an ideal world, states with more food insecurity would have more food banks per capita. This scatter plot tests that assumption — and reveals surprising gaps.

Some of the most food-insecure states (Mississippi, Louisiana) have below-average food bank density. Meanwhile, well-resourced states like Minnesota and Vermont have dense networks despite lower insecurity rates.

Source ProPublica (orgs) + Feeding America (insecurity) Bubble Size Total revenue of food assistance orgs
The correlation between need and resources is weak — infrastructure often follows wealth, not hunger.

Following the Money

Food assistance is a $48 billion industry. California, Texas, and New York alone account for over $15 billion in combined revenue. These numbers include all food assistance nonprofits classified under IRS NTEE code K31.

Revenue comes from government grants, private donations, corporate partnerships, and program fees. Larger organizations benefit from economies of scale — but smaller pantries often serve the hardest-to-reach communities.

Source ProPublica Nonprofit Explorer, IRS 990 filings Data Year Most recent filing (2022-2023)
The top 5 states by revenue handle 41% of all food assistance funding nationally.

Program Efficiency

Dollars to Meals

The program expense ratio measures how much of every dollar goes directly to feeding people (vs. overhead and fundraising). The national average is 82.4% — meaning 82 cents of every dollar reaches programs.

This radar compares four US regions across five dimensions: efficiency, density, average revenue per org, food insecurity rate, and organization count. The Midwest leads in efficiency, while the South faces the highest need with fewer resources per capita.

Source IRS 990 filings via ProPublica Metric Program expenses / total expenses x 100
A 1% improvement in efficiency across all food banks would redirect $480 million more toward feeding people.

Distribution vs. Need

The Mismatch

This chart compares the food insecurity rate (the need) with food bank density (the supply) for the top 15 most food-insecure states. The gaps reveal where the infrastructure is weakest relative to demand.

States where the orange bar (insecurity) far exceeds the blue bar (density) are the ones where food banks are most stretched — and where technology-driven efficiency matters most.

Source ProPublica + Feeding America Data Year 2022-2023
Mississippi has the highest insecurity rate (18.7%) but below-average food bank density — a clear infrastructure gap.
