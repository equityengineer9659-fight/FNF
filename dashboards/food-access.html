Mapping the Food Deserts

A food desert is a census tract where a significant share of residents live far from a supermarket — more than 1 mile in urban areas or 10 miles in rural areas. This map shows the percentage of each state's census tracts classified as low-access. Click any state to drill down into county-level data.

This measure is computed from current data. We use USDA FNS SNAP-authorized retailer locations (updated biweekly) and Census 2020 population-weighted tract centroids. Only supermarkets, super stores, and large grocery stores count as qualifying access points.

Retailers USDA FNS SNAP Retailer Location Data (current, ~40K qualifying stores of ~260K total) Tracts Census 2020 population-weighted centroids (84,414 tracts) Definition Low-access: >1mi urban / >10mi rural to nearest supermarket/super store/large grocery

SNAP Authorization Does Not Mean Access

Over 267,000 retailers are authorized to accept SNAP benefits nationwide — but authorization alone does not guarantee access. Over half of authorized stores are convenience stores and small groceries, while only about 15% are full-service supermarkets, super stores, or large grocery stores. Over 80% of SNAP dollars are redeemed at these larger stores.

States like Mississippi (123 per 100K) and West Virginia (121 per 100K) have the highest retailer density — but most are small stores with limited fresh food. Meanwhile, Utah (61 per 100K) and California (64 per 100K) have the fewest retailers per capita, yet more are full-service grocers. Density without quality is a false signal.

Source USDA FNS, SNAP Retailer Locator Data Year FY2024 (Dec 2025 retailer file) Metric Authorized SNAP retailers per 100,000 residents
The gap between SNAP-authorized retailers and actual grocery access is one of the most overlooked barriers to food security. A convenience store accepts EBT — but rarely stocks fresh produce.

Urban vs. Rural Food Access

Different Barriers, Same Outcome

Urban food deserts are about density — too many people, too few qualifying stores. Rural food deserts are about distance — the nearest grocery may be 5–10+ miles away. Under a 1-mile urban threshold, 44% of urban tracts are flagged as low-access. Only 14% of rural tracts fail the 10-mile rural threshold.

But that lower rural rate does not mean a smaller problem. Rural communities face nearly 3× greater distances (4.9 mi avg vs 1.8 mi), have fewer transportation options, and lack the transit infrastructure that mitigates urban access gaps.

Source Computed from USDA FNS SNAP retailer data + Census 2020 tract centroids Metric Low-access rate = share of tracts failing distance threshold; avg distance weighted by tract count
Urban areas have more low-access tracts by rate (44% vs 14%) — but rural communities travel nearly 3× farther. Both require solutions, not the same solution.

Average Distance to Nearest Qualifying Grocery

A Descriptive Access Measure

This chart shows the state average distance from a census tract's population center to the nearest qualifying store — defined as supermarkets, super stores, or large grocery stores from the USDA FNS SNAP retailer dataset. It is a descriptive measure of geographic proximity, not a threshold-based food desert classification.

State averages smooth over significant internal variation: rural counties within a state can far exceed the average. Alaska (26.8 mi avg) is capped at 10 mi on the chart scale to preserve readability for the lower 48 — hover to see the actual value.

Source Computed from USDA FNS SNAP retailer data + Census 2020 tract centroids Qualifying stores Supermarket, Super Store, Large Grocery Store (from FNS retailer type field) Method Haversine distance from population-weighted tract centroid to nearest qualifying store
South Dakota averages 7.0 miles — more than 6× the distance in New Jersey (1.1 mi). Alaska at 26.8 mi reflects vast frontier geography with few qualifying retailers.

Distance Is the Primary Food Access Barrier

Physical Distance Defines Access

States with greater average distances to grocery stores consistently have higher food desert rates. This is the most direct physical measure of access: when stores are far, communities are underserved.

Dense urban states (DC, New Jersey) have low average distances — proximity reduces access barriers regardless of income. Rural western and plains states (Wyoming, Montana, North Dakota) see the longest distances, reflecting the sparse retail geography that often means long trips for basic groceries.

Unlike car ownership or income, distance is a structural barrier that policy can address through retailer incentive programs, mobile markets, and rural infrastructure investment.

Source Custom current low-access computation: Haversine distance from Census tract centroids to nearest SNAP-authorized supermarket/grocery (FNS retailer data + Census 2020 tract geography), computed 2026 Note State-level averages mask within-state variation; rural counties within otherwise urban states may show much longer distances
Wyoming averages over 4 miles to the nearest grocery store — and 60% of its tracts qualify as low-access. Distance is the defining structural barrier in food-scarce states.

Where Poverty and Low Access Overlap

Living in a low-access area is hard. Living in a low-access area while also being low-income is associated with compounded barriers to affordable food access. These households face higher transportation, delivery, and price constraints.

Texas leads with 1.8 million people in this overlap, followed by Florida and California. But as a share of population, states like Mississippi and West Virginia are most severely affected.

Source Current low-access computation (FNS retailer data + Census 2020 tract geography, computed 2026) combined with state poverty rates from USDA ERS / Feeding America 2024 Metric Modeled estimate: low-access population × state poverty rate. This is a proxy, not a direct count of individuals who are both low-income and low-access. Actual tract-level overlap may vary.
Millions of Americans face overlapping low access and low income — these communities face the greatest compounded access and affordability constraints.

Housing Cost Burden & Food Access

When Rent Crowds Out Food

Renters paying more than 50% of their income on housing have little left for food. Combined with limited vehicle access and distant grocery stores, severe housing cost burden is associated with compounding barriers to food security.

This chart uses 2023 Census ACS data alongside current food desert measures to show how housing cost pressures compound food access challenges.

Source U.S. Census ACS 5-Year Estimates 2023 (housing) + Current food desert tracts (FNS retailers + Census 2020)

Low Access and Food Insecurity

Correlation, Not Causation

This chart compares state-level low-access prevalence with food insecurity rates across all 50 states. The relationship is positive but weak — suggesting that low-access prevalence alone explains little of the variation in food insecurity at the state level.

If low access were a dominant state-level driver, we would expect a stronger positive relationship. The actual correlation suggests that income, employment, and systemic poverty likely play a major role — though state averages can mask stronger county- or neighborhood-level patterns. Switch to County View to drill into individual states with ~60-250 data points, using CHR food insecurity rates (y-axis) and current food desert tract data (x-axis).

Source Current food desert tracts (FNS + Census), CHR 2025, Feeding America
Note State-level analysis; low-access definition uses distance-only threshold — county or tract-level patterns may differ
