Mapping the Food Deserts

A food desert is a census tract where a significant share of residents live far from a supermarket — more than 1 mile in urban areas or 10 miles in rural areas. This map shows the percentage of each state's census tracts classified as low-access. Click any state to drill down into county-level poverty rates as a proxy for food access risk.

Mississippi (23.4%), West Virginia (22.8%), and Alaska (22.5%) have the highest rates — among seven states where more than 20% of census tracts are food deserts. Dense northeastern states fare better due to proximity, not necessarily affordability.

Source USDA ERS, Food Access Research Atlas Data Year 2019 (most recent atlas) Definition Low-access: >1mi urban / >10mi rural to supermarket
23.5 million Americans live in food deserts — including 6.7 million children.

Urban vs. Rural Food Access

Different Barriers, Same Outcome

Urban food deserts are about density — too many people, too few stores. Rural food deserts are about distance — the nearest grocery may be 20+ miles away. Both leave families without consistent access to fresh, healthy food.

Nationally, the split is roughly even — but the barriers are different. Urban tracts need more stores; rural tracts need shorter distances. Hover for the exact breakdown.

Source USDA ERS, Food Access Research Atlas Metric Count of low-access census tracts (urban vs rural)
Rural food deserts often get overlooked — but they affect some of the poorest communities in America.

Distance to Nearest Supermarket

How Far Is Too Far?

The USDA defines food deserts differently for urban (>1 mile) and rural (>10 miles) areas. But even the average distance tells a story: in Wyoming and Montana, residents travel over 5 miles on average to reach a supermarket.

For families without reliable transportation, even 2 miles can be an insurmountable barrier — especially when carrying groceries home on foot or by bus.

Source USDA ERS, Food Access Research Atlas Metric Average distance to nearest supermarket (miles)
Wyoming residents travel an average of 5.5 miles to the nearest grocery store — more than 6x the distance in New Jersey (0.9 mi).

Vehicle Access & Food Deserts

No Car, No Food?

Transportation is the invisible barrier. In New York City, 28% of households lack a car — but dense transit networks mitigate the impact. In rural Mississippi, 8% lack vehicles and there is no public transit at all.

This scatter plot reveals that the worst outcomes occur where both vehicle access is low and food desert prevalence is high — a combination concentrated in the rural South.

Source USDA ERS Food Access Research Atlas (vehicle access + food deserts) Data Year 2019
Mississippi has both the highest food desert rate (23.4%) and one of the highest no-vehicle rates (8.2%) outside of cities.

Where Poverty and Food Deserts Overlap

Living in a food desert is hard. Living in a food desert while also being low-income is a crisis. These families cannot afford to drive farther, pay delivery fees, or shop at premium stores. They are trapped.

Texas leads with 1.7 million people in this overlap, followed by California and Florida. But as a share of population, states like Mississippi and West Virginia are most severely affected.

Source USDA ERS, Food Access Research Atlas Metric Population in low-income + low-access census tracts
Over 17 million Americans live at the intersection of poverty and food deserts — the hardest communities to reach.

Housing Cost Burden & Food Access

When Rent Crowds Out Food

Renters paying more than 50% of their income on housing have little left for food. Combined with limited vehicle access and distant grocery stores, severe housing cost burden creates a compounding barrier to food security.

This chart uses 2023 Census ACS data alongside the 2019 food desert atlas (now 7 years old) to show how current housing pressures overlap with food access challenges.

Source U.S. Census ACS 5-Year Estimates 2023 (housing) + USDA Food Access Atlas 2019 (food deserts)
Updated Housing data refreshes annually via Census API

Do Food Deserts Cause Hunger?

Correlation, Not Causation

The assumption that food deserts directly cause food insecurity is common in policy debates — but the data tells a more nuanced story. This scatter tests the relationship between food desert prevalence (low-access tracts) and food insecurity rate across all 50 states.

If food deserts were the primary driver, we'd expect a strong positive correlation. The actual relationship reveals whether income, employment, and systemic poverty are more powerful predictors than geographic access alone.

Source USDA Food Access Atlas 2019 + USDA ERS/Feeding America 2022
Note Access data is 3 years older than insecurity data — interpret correlation with caution
